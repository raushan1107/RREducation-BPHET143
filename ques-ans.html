(a) Concept of hole in semiconductor covalent bonding ▶

In semiconductors like Silicon or Germanium, atoms are held together by covalent bonds, where neighboring atoms share valence electrons.

  • Formation of a Hole: At room temperature, thermal energy can cause some valence electrons to break free from their covalent bonds and become free electrons in the conduction band.
  • Concept of a Hole: When an electron leaves its covalent bond, it leaves behind an empty valence electron state. This empty state is called a hole.
  • Effective Positive Charge: A hole acts as a positive charge because the removal of an electron creates a net positive charge in that region of the crystal lattice.
  • Mobility: Other electrons from adjacent covalent bonds can move into this empty hole, effectively causing the hole to move in the opposite direction of the electron's movement. This movement of holes contributes to current flow, similar to the movement of positive charges.

(f) Barkhausen criterion for sustained oscillations ▶

The Barkhausen criterion specifies the conditions for an electronic oscillator circuit to produce continuous, self-sustained oscillations without an external input signal:

  • Loop Gain Condition: The magnitude of the loop gain (∣Aβ∣) must be equal to or greater than unity (i.e., ∣Aβ∣≥1). 'A' is amplifier gain, 'β' is feedback network gain.
  • Phase Shift Condition: The total phase shift around the closed loop (ϕloop) must be 0 degrees or an integer multiple of 360 degrees (i.e., ϕloop = 2nπ radians or 360n degrees, where n = 0, 1, 2...).

(a) Formation of p-n junction and depletion region; barrier potential ▶

Formation of p-n Junction: A p-n junction is formed when a p-type semiconductor (excess holes) is fused adjacent to an n-type semiconductor (excess free electrons).

Formation of Depletion Region:

  • Diffusion: Electrons from n-side diffuse to p-side, and holes from p-side diffuse to n-side due to concentration gradient.
  • Recombination: Diffusing electrons and holes recombine.
  • Immobile Ions: Electrons leaving n-side leave behind positive donor ions (ND+). Holes filled on p-side leave behind negative acceptor ions (NA-). These ions are fixed.
  • Depletion Layer: This process creates a region around the junction depleted of mobile charge carriers, leaving only immobile ions.

(A labelled diagram showing the p-type and n-type regions, the junction, the immobile positive ions in the n-side of the depletion region, and immobile negative ions in the p-side of the depletion region, with arrows indicating electron/hole diffusion, would greatly enhance the answer.)

Barrier Potential (or Potential Barrier/Voltage Barrier):

  • Definition: The immobile ions in the depletion region create an electric field, establishing a potential difference that opposes further diffusion of majority carriers.
  • Polarity: The n-side of the depletion region becomes positive, and the p-side becomes negative.
(b) Labelled output characteristics of BJT in CE configuration ▶

(Draw a graph with Collector Current (IC) on the Y-axis and Collector-Emitter Voltage (VCE) on the X-axis. Include multiple curves, each representing a different fixed value of Base Current (IB). Clearly label the three regions: Cutoff, Active, and Saturation.)

Output Characteristics of BJT in Common Emitter (CE) Configuration:

  • Active Region:
    • Features: IC is almost independent of VCE and proportional to IB. BE junction forward-biased, CB junction reverse-biased.
    • Application: Used for amplification of AC signals.
  • Cutoff Region:
    • Features: IB is zero or very small. Both BE and CB junctions are reverse-biased. IC is negligible ("off").
    • Application: Used as an open switch in digital logic circuits.
  • Saturation Region:
    • Features: IB is large, both BE and CB junctions are forward-biased. IC reaches maximum. VCE is very small (0.1V to 0.3V).
    • Application: Used as a closed switch in digital logic circuits.
(c) Universal biasing of n-p-n transistor in CE configuration and Q-point stabilization ▶

Universal Biasing (Voltage Divider Biasing): Most widely used and stable biasing method for BJT amplifiers.

(Draw a circuit diagram of an n-p-n transistor in CE configuration with voltage divider biasing. This includes resistors R1 and R2 forming a voltage divider at the base, a collector resistor RC, and an emitter resistor RE, all connected to a single DC supply VCC. Include a bypass capacitor across RE for AC signals, though for DC biasing explanation, it's optional.)

Explanation:

  • Base Voltage Division: R1 and R2 set a stable DC voltage at the base (VB).
  • Emitter Voltage and Current: Emitter resistor RE provides negative feedback. VE = IE × RE ≈ VB - VBE, determining IE.
  • Collector Current: Since IC ≈ IE, IC is also set.
  • Collector-Emitter Voltage: VC = VCC - ICRC, and VCE = VC - VE, establishing the Q-point.

Q-point Stabilization in Universal Biasing:

The emitter resistor (RE) stabilizes the Q-point against temperature and transistor β variations:

  • Temperature Increase / β Increase: IC tends to increase.
  • Increased Emitter Current: IE increases.
  • Increased Voltage Drop across RE: VE = IE × RE increases.
  • Reduced Base-Emitter Voltage (VBE): VBE = VB - VE decreases (since VB is fixed).
  • Reduced Base Current (IB): Decreased VBE reduces IB.
  • Stabilized Collector Current: Reduced IB counteracts the initial IC increase, stabilizing the Q-point through negative feedback.

4. Attempt any two parts: ▶
(a) Current gain of CE amplifier in terms of h-parameters ▶

For a Common Emitter (CE) amplifier, the current gain (Ai) is the ratio of output collector current (IC) to input base current (IB). Using the h-parameter model:

VBE = hieIB + hreVCE

IC = hfeIB + hoeVCE

Where:

  • hie: input impedance
  • hre: reverse voltage transfer ratio
  • hfe: forward current transfer ratio (βac)
  • hoe: output admittance

Considering load resistance rL: VCE = -ICrL

Substitute VCE into the second equation:

IC = hfeIB + hoe(-ICrL)
IC = hfeIB - hoeICrL
IC(1 + hoerL) = hfeIB

Finally, the current gain (Ai = IC/IB) is:

Ai = hfe / (1 + hoerL)

This expression shows that the current gain depends on hfe, hoe, and the load resistance rL.

5. Attempt any two parts: ▶
(a) Op-amp parameters and ideal values: CMRR, Input offset voltage ▶
  • (i) CMRR (Common Mode Rejection Ratio):
    • Explanation: Measures an op-amp's ability to reject common-mode signals (noise appearing identically on both inputs) while amplifying differential signals. High CMRR means effective noise suppression.
    • Ideal Value: Infinity (∞).
  • (ii) Input offset voltage (Vio):
    • Explanation: The small, unwanted DC voltage that appears at the output when both inputs are at the same potential (ideally 0V output). It's the differential input voltage needed to nullify this output.
    • Ideal Value: 0 V.
(b) Basic integrator using op-amp and output voltage expression ▶

Circuit Diagram of a Basic Op-amp Integrator:

(Draw a circuit diagram: An Op-Amp with the non-inverting input grounded. An input resistor (Rin) connects the input voltage (Vin) to the inverting input. A capacitor (Cf) is connected in the feedback path between the output and the inverting input. The output voltage is Vout.)

Expression for Output Voltage:

Assuming ideal op-amp characteristics and virtual ground at inverting input:

  • Input Current (Iin): Iin = Vin / Rin
  • Feedback Current (If): If = Iin = -Cf (dVout/dt)

Equating currents and solving for Vout:

Vout(t) = - (1 / (RinCf)) ∫ Vin(τ)dτ + Vinitial

The output voltage is proportional to the negative integral of the input voltage, scaled by the time constant RinCf.

(e) Units for h-parameters ▶
  • (i) h21 (hf): Forward current transfer ratio (e.g., hfe). It is a ratio of output current to input current (ΔIout/ΔIin). Unit: Dimensionless (no units).
  • (ii) h11 (hi): Input impedance (e.g., hie). It is a ratio of input voltage to input current (ΔVin/ΔIin). Unit: Ohms (Ω).
(f) PIV of diodes in full wave rectifiers ▶

PIV (Peak Inverse Voltage) is the maximum reverse-bias voltage a diode can withstand without breakdown.

  • (i) Full-Wave Rectifier with Centre-Tapped Transformer: PIV for each diode is 2Vm (where Vm is peak voltage from center tap to either end).
  • (ii) Full-Wave Bridge Rectifier: PIV for each diode is Vm (where Vm is the peak secondary voltage of the transformer).
(g) Ideal Op-amp characteristics ▶

For an ideal Operational Amplifier (Op-amp):

  • (i) Input offset voltage: 0 V
  • (ii) CMRR (Common Mode Rejection Ratio): Infinity (∞)
  • (iii) Output resistance: 0 Ω
  • (iv) Slew rate: Infinity (∞)
(h) Why trigger pulse for monostable IC 555 should be narrow ▶

In monostable mode, the IC 555 timer produces a single output pulse in response to a negative-going edge on the trigger input (Pin 2).

A very narrow trigger pulse ensures that the 555 timer registers a clear, distinct falling edge and then begins its timing cycle unhindered. If the pulse is too wide or stays LOW for too long, the timer can malfunction, falsely re-trigger, or produce an unstable output, preventing it from completing its intended single, stable output pulse.

(a) Working of an LED with energy band diagram; I-V characteristics region ▶

Working of an LED (Light Emitting Diode) with Energy Band Diagram:

(Draw an energy band diagram for a forward-biased p-n junction, showing the conduction band and valence band for both p-type and n-type materials, the Fermi levels, and the energy gap.   • Semiconductor Material: LEDs are direct bandgap p-n junction diodes (e.g., GaAsP, GaN).
  • Forward Biasing: LED must be forward-biased (positive to p-region, negative to n-region).
  • Electron-Hole Injection: Under forward bias, electrons from n-side inject into p-region, holes from p-side inject into n-region.
  • Recombination and Photon Emission: Injected minority carriers recombine in the depletion region. Electron falling from conduction band to fill a hole in valence band releases energy as a photon (light).
  • Light Color: Determined by the energy bandgap (Eg) of the semiconductor material.

Region of I-V Characteristics for Operation:

LEDs operate exclusively in the forward-biased region of their I-V characteristics, where current conducts after the threshold voltage is overcome, and light emission occurs.

(b) Common Base (CB), Common Emitter (CE), and Common Collector (CC) configurations of n-p-n transistor ▶

Transistors have three primary configurations based on which terminal is common to both input and output circuits:

(For each configuration, draw a basic circuit diagram of an n-p-n transistor showing the input, output, and the common terminal clearly. Include biasing resistors and DC supply for completeness.)

  • Common Base (CB) Configuration:
    • Description: Base terminal is common to input (emitter-base) and output (collector-base). Input: Emitter-Base, Output: Collector-Base.
    • Characteristics: High Voltage Gain, Current Gain (α) < 1, Low Input Resistance, High Output Resistance, No Phase Inversion (0°).
    • Applications: High-frequency applications, impedance matching (low to high).
  • Common Emitter (CE) Configuration:
    • Description: Emitter terminal is common to input (base-emitter) and output (collector-emitter). Input: Base-Emitter, Output: Collector-Emitter.
    • Characteristics: High Voltage Gain, High Current Gain (β), Medium Input Resistance, Medium Output Resistance, Phase Inversion (180°).
    • Applications: General-purpose voltage amplifier, audio frequency (AF) amplifiers.
  • Common Collector (CC) Configuration (Emitter Follower):
    • Description: Collector terminal is common to input (base-collector) and output (emitter-collector). Input: Base-Collector, Output: Emitter.
    • Characteristics: Voltage Gain < 1 (close to 1), High Current Gain, Very High Input Resistance, Very Low Output Resistance, No Phase Inversion (0°).
    • Applications: Impedance matching (high to low), current buffering, current amplification.
